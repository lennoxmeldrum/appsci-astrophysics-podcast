Requirements

Core requirements

1) A clear driving question

Start with 1â€“3 big questions that genuinely guide the episode (unknowns, debates, recent discoveries, limitations). The episode should feel like it is investigating something, not reading a Wikipedia page.

2) Evidence-based explanation

Build claims with support. Bring in credible sources (scientific papers, expert interviews, reputable organisations). When you present a claim, explain why the evidence supports it.

3) Source quality and transparency

Use reliable sources and cross-check key ideas. Be honest about uncertainty and what scientists still do not know. Keep track of sources as you go.

4) Accessible communication

Explain terminology. Use analogies carefully (accurate, not misleading). Your goal is clarity, not sounding impressive.

5) Production quality

Clear audio matters. Use music or sound effects only if they support the learning and do not distract.

Structure

Suggested episode structure

You do not have to follow this exactly, but your episode must have a deliberate structure.

1) Hook (0:00â€“0:45)

A surprising fact, mini-story, or question that earns attention.

2) Framing and driving question (0:45â€“1:30)

What is this episode trying to figure out? Why does it matter?

3) Background and current understanding (1:30â€“6:00)

4) The interesting part â€” uncertainty, controversy, or frontier (6:00â€“12:00)

5) Engagement moment (anywhere)

A thought experiment, hypothetical question, or short interactive moment for listeners.

6) Conclusion (final 1â€“2 minutes)

Research and Sources

Great podcasts are built on great sources. You will create an annotated bibliography that supports your episode with both listenable context and reliable scientific grounding.

Requirements

Source requirement

Your group research base must include at least 4 sources:

2 podcast sources

  • These can be episodes from credible science outlets (not random opinion shows)
  • Use them for explanations, analogies, and framing
  • You still must cross-check key claims with non-podcast sources

2 non-podcast sources

  • Peer-reviewed papers, textbooks, university resources, NASA/ESA, reputable research organisations
  • Use these to verify claims and build accurate explanations
  • At least 1 should be a primary or research-grade source if possible
Rule: A podcast is not proof. If it matters, verify it.
Evaluation

How to judge source quality

Use these fast checks (you do not need every box, but you do need to show you thought about reliability).

Credibility

  • Who made it? Are they qualified?
  • Are references provided (or do they cite researchers and studies)?
  • Is the organisation reputable?

Two-source test

  • Do at least 2 credible sources agree on the key claim?
  • If they disagree, can you explain why (different models, uncertainties, older data)?

Relevance

  • Does it actually help answer your driving question?
  • Does it provide a usable example, explanation, or data?

Bias and uncertainty

  • Is it trying to persuade rather than explain?
  • Does it acknowledge limits, uncertainty, or open questions?
